Reading passage

India's pharmaceutical industry has emerged as a global leader in healthcare, providing a significant portion of the world's generic medications and vaccines. Through strategic government initiatives like the Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes, the nation has boosted the domestic manufacture of raw materials, high-end drugs, and medical devices. These policies, alongside increased foreign investment, have driven massive growth, making essential treatments up to 80% more affordable for the public. The establishment of specialised drug parks and modernised laboratories ensures that the sector remains competitive and innovative on an international scale. Ultimately, these advancements have successfully bridged the gap between urban manufacturing hubs and rural accessibility, fostering both economic development and improved medical outcomes. This transformation solidifies India's role as a vital supplier of high-quality, low-cost medicine for both its own citizens and the global population.

Discussion Questions
  • 1 India supplies a large share of the world's generic medicines and vaccines. Do you think wealthy countries should rely on a single nation for so much of their medicine supply? What are the potential risks?
  • 2 The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme encourages companies to manufacture more within India by offering financial rewards. What are the advantages and disadvantages of governments using financial incentives to shape industry?
  • 3 The text mentions that India's pharmaceutical growth has improved rural accessibility to medicines. In your experience or opinion, what other barriers — beyond cost — prevent people in rural areas from accessing healthcare?
  • 4 India is described as a "vital supplier of high-quality, low-cost medicine" for the global population. How important is it for low- and middle-income countries to develop their own pharmaceutical industries rather than importing medicines from abroad?
